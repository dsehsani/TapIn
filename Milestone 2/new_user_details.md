## Raw Notes from Interviews:

## 1: Interview with UC Davis Student: Jina Hwang [Version 3]
    Phase 1: The Setup (lower the stakes)
    
    "I’m going to show you a rough prototype we’re working on. I want to be clear: I am testing the app, I am not testing you. If you get stuck or confused, that is great — it helps me fix the design. Please be honest; you won’t hurt my feelings."

    Phase 2: The "First Glance" (The 5-Second Test)
    
    Handed device on home screen. Did NOT give a task yet.
    "Take a look at this screen for a few seconds. Without clicking anything yet... what do you think this app does?"

    Response:
    “I think it’s showing events and things that are happening, not just in UC Davis, but also in the community of Davis as a whole. Maybe even some social commentary as well. There’s some conversations and hot topics about what’s going on in the world as well. It definitely seems like a news app that is targeting what Davis students or residents may find interesting.”

    Phase 3: The Task (The Core Loop)

    Scenario given, not instructions.
    Script: "Imagine you are planning a trip for next weekend. Show me how you would use this tool to get a list of restaurants."
    Silently observed usage.
    Confusion = key focus area.
    
    TASK GIVEN:
    “You just heard about this app that allows you to find what’s going on in Davis. Show me how you would find a campus event and save it, then find the saved event.”
    
    Observations / Notes:
    Scrolled through news first.
    Events tab easily located.
    AI summaries seem to be helpful.
    Event found easily.
    “Save it?” — discrepancy between “Save” and “Attend” is strange.
    Says “Attend” on events, but inconsistent with “Saved Events” language.
    “Saved” tab is not obvious.
    Went to profile, tried to interact with the saved event.
    “I feel like in terms of stories, I would use this button to save.”
    “If I saved an event, would I get reminded by the app about the event? I feel like if I save it, I would forget, especially something well in advance.”
    Suggestion: calendar integration.

    Phase 4: The AI "Vibe Check"
    Tested quality of AI response, not just buttons.
    Questions asked:
    "Look at the answer the AI just gave you."
    "Is this useful?"
    "Do you trust it?"
    "What would you do with this information next?"
    
    Homepage:
    “I don’t know if it’s useful, but it does its job so it’s helpful. Maybe because I’m not as interested in these topics, but it summarizes the page well in a short and digestible way. If there were something that would catch my eye, this is a good way to see it.”
    “Yes.”
    “If there’s something that caught my eye, I’d go through the homepage to read more about the event.”
    
    Events:
    “Yes, these are really helpful, I would 100% use these. I like how I don’t have to click on it compared to the homepage.”
    “Yes, I do trust it, but I also know that because it’s a summary, it’s not able to fully encompass everything.”
    “I would use it to get the gist of the event in general to see if it would interest me.”

    Phase 5: The Debrief

    Questions:
    "On a scale of 1 to 5, how difficult was that?"
    "If you had a magic wand, what is the one thing you would change to make this easier?"
    "How would you describe this app to a friend in one sentence?"

    Responses:
    Saving the event: 1
    Finding the event: maybe a 2 or 3
    “I feel like I had to look a little harder for events. If that’s something we were trying to push, making it an option that is higher priority to access rather than in the bottom would be helpful.”
    “A news app for Davis residents and students to tune into things going on in the area as well as hot topics in the community.”

## 9: Interview with Kiran Bangalore (Guest Professor) [Version 1]
    Phase 1: The Setup (lower the stakes)

    Asked about target audience:
    Are we getting professors?
    Other UCD employees?
    Grad students?
    Noted onboarding options: freshman/sophomore/junior/senior/grad.
    Asked: What is the experience you want a user to have when walking away from the app?
    “Knowledge about Davis” — but what task is completed?
    If someone thinks a water polo game is interesting, they should know where to go.
    The game should be happening in the future.

    Phase 2: The "First Glance" (The 5-Second Test)
    RSS issue — renders “ugly,” requires code cleanup.
    “What’s happening today” is the same as bottom of News section.
    AI summary is too wordy — he didn’t read any of it.
    Spacing should be fixed.
    Aesthetics of summary should be more readable.

    Phase 3: The Task (The Core Loop)
    Difficulty figuring out how to save something — be consistent with saving icons.
    “Save” vs “Attend” inconsistency.
    Recommended tuning “What’s happening today” into a calendar format with times.
    Bring some event to the front based on user preferences.
    TikTok-style short 3-question personalization quiz during onboarding.
    Example: bring free food or live music info to the user’s front page.
    That gets people to come back everyday.
    Wordle keyboard has non-obvious coloring — darker letters should be eliminated ones.

    Phase 4: The AI "Vibe Check"
    PERSONALIZATION:
    Use LLM to summarize what is happening based on the user’s preferences.
    AI summary too long.
    Needs better spacing + readability.
    Mentioned possibly rotating API key once a month and having people re-sign up each month (security / engagement angle).

    Phase 5: The Debrief
    Not sold on games.
    Yes, community building.
    But doesn’t think people will come to the app to play games.
    Why not build community with large-scale campus events?
    Games are not primary — wouldn’t give it the center item.
    Primary aspect of community building is events.
    Focus on things people will pay for.
    
    Monetization strategy:
    Make this the place to buy tickets to events.
    Middle icon should be “Events.”
    Think about position of icons on apps.
